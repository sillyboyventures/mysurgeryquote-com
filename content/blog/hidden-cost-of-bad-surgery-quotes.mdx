Cosmetic surgeons treat quoting as a 15-minute administrative task. The real cost is much higher than 15 minutes. It shows up in labor hours, in lost patients, and in fee mistakes that quietly eat margin. Here's the math.

## The time math

Take a mid-sized practice running 18 quotes a week. Building a quote with a spreadsheet, a calculator, and the EMR's PDF export takes about 12 minutes each. That's 3.6 hours a week. Over a year, 180 hours — most of it a coordinator's time.

At a loaded coordinator cost of $40 an hour, that's **$7,200 a year** of pure quote-building labor. Not consults. Not follow-up. Just assembling quotes.

The same practice on MySurgeryQuote builds a quote in about 2 minutes. Eighteen quotes a week is 30 hours a year — roughly **$1,200** in labor. Net savings: about **$6,000 a year**, before you count a single extra booked case.

## The conversion math

Cosmetic patients shop. They request quotes from two to four practices before booking. The practice with the cleanest, most professional, most itemized quote wins more often than the practice that emails a stitched-together estimate three days later.

Industry estimates put quote-to-close conversion in cosmetic surgery somewhere between 30% and 50%. Suppose a faster, more professional quote lifts your close rate by five points. On 18 quotes a week, that's roughly 47 additional booked procedures a year. At an average case value of $8,000, that's about **$376,000** in additional revenue.

Treat that number as directional, not a promise. Conversion impact varies widely by market, procedure mix, and consult quality — use your own close rate and case value. Even at a fraction of that estimate, the revenue dwarfs the software cost.

## The mistake math

Manual fee calculation introduces errors. A facility-fee miscalculation of $500 happens often enough — call it 1 quote in 20, a conservative rate. On 18 quotes a week, that's about 47 errored quotes a year.

Say half get caught before the quote goes out. The other half leave you two bad options:

- An awkward conversation: "We miscalculated — the price is actually $500 more than we told you."
- An absorbed loss: you honor the wrong number and eat the $500.

Either way, that's real money, and the first option costs trust at the exact moment a patient is deciding. [Automatic facility and anesthesia math](/features/#facility-fees) removes the category of error entirely.
